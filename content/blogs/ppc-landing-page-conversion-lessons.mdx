I've spent the last few years building PPC landing pages for US-based clients as part of an internal marketing web team — well over 200 of them at this point. Different industries, different ad platforms, same underlying job: someone clicks an ad, lands on a page, and either converts or bounces in the next few seconds.

No client names or numbers here — that work is under an internal agreement, and I'm not going to dress up vague memories as hard data anyway. What I can talk about is the patterns that kept showing up, page after page, regardless of the industry on top.

## The brief always says "make it convert." The page rarely says how.

Most landing page briefs hand you a headline, a few bullet points, and a CTA label, then leave the actual conversion mechanics up to you. Early on I treated that as a design problem — better hero image, snappier copy, a bigger button. Some of that matters. Most of it doesn't move the needle nearly as much as the structural stuff nobody puts in the brief.

Three things consistently mattered more than visual polish:

1. **How fast the page answers "is this for me?"** — not the headline alone, the whole first screen.
2. **How many decisions the visitor has to make before the CTA.**
3. **Whether the form (if there is one) matches the actual commitment level the visitor is ready for.**

I'll go through each.

## Speed-to-relevance beats clever copy

PPC traffic is cold and impatient in a specific way — they already clicked an ad with a promise on it, and they're checking whether the landing page honors that promise. If the first screen doesn't visibly match the ad's claim, they're gone before they read your second paragraph.

This is why **message match** matters more than headline cleverness. If the ad says "Free Shipping on Orders Over $50," and the landing page headline doesn't mention shipping anywhere above the fold, you've already lost a chunk of that traffic — not because the page is bad, but because it broke the promise the ad made.

<Callout type="tip" title="Quick audit">
  Screenshot your ad and your landing page hero side by side. If you can't immediately see the
  same claim in both, that's the first thing to fix — before touching colors, fonts, or layout.
</Callout>

## Every extra decision is a chance to lose someone

A landing page with five nav links, three CTAs, and a "maybe later" exit-intent popup is asking the visitor to make decisions they didn't come there to make. PPC traffic arrived with one intent already formed by the ad. The page's job is to get out of the way of that intent, not add new branches to it.

In practice this means:

- **Cut the nav.** A landing page rarely needs a full site nav — it usually needs a logo (for trust) and one path forward.
- **One primary CTA, repeated, not varied.** Don't offer "Buy Now," "Learn More," and "Get a Quote" on the same page expecting the visitor to pick the right one. Pick one action and repeat it.
- **Kill the popups that compete with the page's own goal.** An exit-intent discount popup that pulls attention away from your main offer is solving for "anything is better than nothing," which isn't the same as solving for conversion.

I built a few self-initiated landing page samples that apply this directly — single CTA, no nav, the offer repeated once at the top and once at the bottom. You can see one of them on the [projects page](/projects).

## Match the form to the commitment level

This is the one I see violated most often, including in pages I built early on before I'd internalized it: the form asks for more than the visitor is ready to give at that point in the funnel.

If someone just clicked a cold awareness-stage ad, asking for phone number, company size, and a "tell us about your project" textarea before they've gotten any value back is a mismatch. They don't trust you yet — you haven't earned that level of ask.

A rough mental model that's served me well:

| Funnel stage              | What the visitor will tolerate giving      |
| -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Cold (first touch from ad) | Email, maybe a URL — nothing else           |
| Warm (retargeting, repeat visitor) | Name, email, basic qualifying question |
| Hot (already requested a quote/demo) | Phone, detailed project info       |

Most of the underperforming pages I've worked on weren't ugly. They were asking cold traffic for a hot-traffic level of commitment.

## Visual polish still matters — just not first

None of this means design doesn't matter. A page that nails message match, decision economy, and form-to-funnel matching but looks unfinished will still underperform a polished one. But I've stopped treating visual work as the first lever to pull. It's the multiplier on top of a structure that's already sound — not a substitute for it.

The order I actually work in now:

1. Confirm message match against the ad.
2. Strip the page down to one path.
3. Match the form to the funnel stage.
4. *Then* spend time on hierarchy, imagery, and motion.

If you do step 4 before 1–3, you end up with a beautiful page that's solving the wrong problem.

## A practical takeaway

If you only take one thing from this: before you touch a single pixel of an underperforming landing page, screenshot the ad next to the page and count how many decisions sit between the headline and the CTA. Most conversion problems I've actually fixed started with answers to those two checks, not a redesign.
